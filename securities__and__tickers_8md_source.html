00001 Securities, Symbols, Tickers, oh my!             {#securities-vs-symbols}
00002 ====================================
00003 
00004 The ITCH-5.0 protocol suffers from a common confusion in the
00005 securities industry.  It calls their ticker field 'Stock'.  While it
00006 is true that most securities traded on Nasdaq are common stock
00007 securities, many are not: Nasdaq also trades Exchange Traded Funds,
00008 Exchange Traded Notes, and even Warrants.
00009 
00010 And the name of a security, i.e., the string used to identify the
00011 security in the many electronic protocols used between exchanges and
00012 participants, is not the same as the security.  A more appropriate
00013 name would have been 'Security Identifier', or 'Ticker', or 'Symbol'.
00014 
00015 Software engineers in the industry should be warned to not confuse a
00016 thing (a security: a contract granting specific rights to its owner),
00017 vs. the name of the thing (a ticker: a short string used to identify a
00018 security).
00019 
00020 Also, software engineers in the field should be aware that the same
00021 security may be identified by different strings in different contexts,
00022 e.g. many exchanges used different tickers for the same security, yes,
00023 even in the US markets.  That sometimes the same tickers refers to
00024 different securities as time goes by, for example, after some
00025 corporate actions.  That in non-US markets it is common to use fairly
00026 long strings (ISINs) or just numbers (in Japan) to identify
00027 securities.  And that the string used to identify securities in a
00028 market feed may not be the same string used to identify them when
00029 placing orders, clearing, etc.
